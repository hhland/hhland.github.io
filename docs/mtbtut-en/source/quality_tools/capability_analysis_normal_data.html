Data requirements for Capability Analysis-Normal  

What kind of data is required?

Your measurement data must be numeric and continuous. For example:
  • Diameter of an engine camshaft in centimeters
  • Volume of juice bottles in milliliters
  • Length of a fan blade in inches

The data should follow the normal distribution, although you can analyze nonnormal data by applying a transformation.

Subgroups

Denote your rational subgroupsthe "batches" of output that share common-cause variation (output from the same time interval, the same method, and so on)if you have them. Examples include:
  • Ten light bulbs selected from each box.
  • Six calls monitored from each hour.

Lumping measurements together from different subgroups may bury important information about your process' potential.

Some data may not have subgroups, such as the results of destructive testing, or measurements taken once a day. For these situations, use a subgroup size of 1.

Control and Stability

The results of a capability analysis are invalid if the process mean and variation are not in statistical control. Use control charts to verify stability.

What should my worksheet look like?

Stack measurements in one column, and use a second column to identify the subgroup of each measurement

If all subgroup sizes are equal, you can omit the column of subscript IDs and simply enter the subgroup size in the dialog box.
