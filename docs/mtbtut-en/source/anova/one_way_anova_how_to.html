Description of how to perform a one-way ANOVA using Minitab  

Scenario

Investigators compared the hardness of four different blends of paint. Six samples of each paint blend were applied to a small piece of metal, cured, and measured for hardness.

Choose the appropriate analysis

A one-way ANOVA is appropriate for a comparison of the means because you have one factor (paint blend) and that factor has more than two levels (four paint blends). You also have a continuous response variable (paint hardness). The data are unstacked because each group's data are in separate columns. Consequently, you choose the unstacked version of the analysis. If the response data had been stacked in one column, you would have selected One-Way.
  1. Open the worksheet PAINT.MTW.
  2. Choose Stat > ANOVA > One-Way.

Enter your data

You have four columns of paint hardness values, one column for each paint type.
  1. Choose Responses data are in a separate column for each factor level.
  2. In Responses, enter 'Blend 1'-'Blend 4'.

Select multiple comparison methods

If you want to estimate the differences between specific groups, select a multiple comparison method. Each method uses a different approach. Choosing the proper method for your needs is very important because the method can change the results. Generally, you choose just one, but, for the sake of illustration, select them all.
  1. Click Comparisons, and check all of the checkboxes under Comparison procedures assuming equal variances.
  2. Under Results, check all of the checkboxes.
  3. Click OK.

Add graphs to your output

The individual value plot and boxplot help you to visualize the central tendency, variation, and distribution of your data. Viewing the residual plots to check the test's assumptions is important for a one-way ANOVA. The plots tell you if you can trust the test results.
  1. Click Graphs, then check Interval plot , Individual value plot, and Boxplot of data.
  2. Under Residual Plots, choose Three in one.
  3. Click OK in each dialog box.
